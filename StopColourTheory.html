IT'S TIME TO STOP COLOUR THEORY
Welcome
COLOUR THEORY is a concept that was contrived in the 18th century by various European thinkers as a means of controlling the way we see the world around us.1

Colour Theory is an intentionally convoluted and enigmatic system that fabricates relationships between colours through systemic classification and hierarchy.

The majority have learned to accept Colour Theory as a universal truth thanks to our flawed education system. Classroom activities are devised to lead children to the conclusion that Colour Theory is a correct and applicable way of understanding and mixing colours.

Furthermore, organisations such as the International Commission on Illumination have made it their goal to keep Colour Theory at the forefront of colour science.

The Fact Society extends an invitation to you to learn and digest the falsehoods and half-truths of Colour Theory and to take the first step towards your journey with COLOUR FACT.
Debunking Colour Theory
Before proceeding with an explanation of the true nature of colour, we would like to dedicate this section to pointing out just a small handful of the inumerable inconsistencies that exist in Colour Theory
Red, yellow, and blue are the primary colours, yet computers use R G B
The mainstream response to this question is that red, green, and blue are, like the traditional primary colours2, a colour palette from which all other visible colours can be derived. This common rhetoric used by scientists, computer engineers, and other colour theorists is fallacious.

Colour Theory admits that yellow and blue produce green while simultaneously demostrating that when using R G B , red and green produce yellow3.

How could combining a primary and secondary colour produce a primary colour? Why is this concept not shown on the colour wheel?
The existence of black
Most people wouldn't flinch when saying that black is a colour, but this hasn't been true for most of human history.

Darkness is the absence of light - yet how can a colour such as black be viewed under natural or artificial light when it is defined as the absence of light? It is strange to consider that our eyes need light to see colours, yet we see black in complete darkness.

It wasn't until the late Middle Ages that the concept of black being a colour began to exist. It was later adopted into subtractive Colour Theory as the final output of mixing all colours together4.

We must ask ourselves whether black is truly a colour, or a concept that exists outside of colour that has been misconstrued in order to legitmise traditional Colour Theory.
The watered-down meaning of 'light colours'
Light and dark, white and black. If these word pairs seem naturally linked, it's likely you have been exposed to Colour Theory. If we read into these words a little more we may begin to notice a slight discord in their relationship.

Think of the darkest, most opaque thing you can; maybe you're think of a deep ditch or a night with no moon, things that could accurately be represented with the colour palette that Colour Theory affords us.

Now think of the lightest, brightest thing you can. You probably thought of the sun. The sun was our first light source and continues to be our fount of daily illumination yet, how is it represented using traditional Colour Theory? Vey poorly.

I have never been blinded by the sun in Van Gogh's Wheatfield with a Reaper or seen a shadow cast in the presence of Starry Night. Most people use light/dark terminology while still believing that colour is limited in its ability to portray actual light. This is one of the many deceptions that Colour Theory has taught a large part of the population not to question.
The primary colours ( red, yellow, and blue ) cannot be obtained through mixing colours
Go to any paint shop and ask for red paint and you will be flooded with different options and vast quantities available. Yet, how can so much paint of a primary colour exist if primary colours can't be created through the mixing of other colours?

Surely there aren't enough red cabbages, berries, and human blood to satisfy the global demand for red dye that is used in the paint, clothing, food colouring, and all its other possible applications. To fish even deeper, how can any primary colour be created by nature assuming traditional Colour Theory?
What is the colour of water?
Colour theorists often portray water as blue, green, or on occasions white. In fact, in China water is considered to be black5! Yet you, reader, know that water is none of those colours. The colour of water is transparent, possibly even reflective.

"But those aren't colours!" you may be thinking. Ask yourself why you believe that transparent and reflective aren't colours and your answer will be that Colour Theory denies their status.

The realm of colour beyond Colour Theory recognises the existence of colours unrestrained by the yoke of the colour wheel. Colour fact is based on unfiltered observation into the realm of colour.
Colours can be defined through frequencies
There exists the belief within Colour Theory that all colours occupy a place within the spectrum of visible light, and that each one emits a certain wavelength in respect to its frequency which we perceive as a certain colour. However, let's delve deeper into the term "frequecy".

Violet occupies the highest frequency of the visible light spectrum with a whopping frequency of 668-789THz6. With such a tremendous frequency, you would expect violet to be everywhere - but in reality, it's not found in the natural world with great prevalence. Lavender, eggplant, grapes, and amethysts are among the few naturally occuring violet things.

Green, on the other hand, should logically have an incredibly high frequency considering that most of the world's landmass is covered in some form of vegetation7, yet it occupies the middle range. What about black that doesn't even appear on the visible light spectrum yet is prevalent throughout the entirety of space and is (claimed to be) a colour?
Mixing all the colours together produces black...or was it white?
What many people fail to realise about traditional Colour Theory is that there are two opposing schools of thought. Subtractive Colour Theory tells us that mixing all colours produces black while additive Colour Theory tells us that mixing all colours produces white 8.

Much like the Great Schism9 divided Christianity, Colour Theory was also divided in its early stages. In 1850, Hermann Von Helmholtz introduced additive Colour Theory to the already existing subtractive Colour Theory10. Whether Helmholtz's creation was an imperfect copy or intentional distinction from traditional Colour Theory is unknown.

However, unlike the branches of Christianity, additive and subtractive Colour Theory are both present in mainstream culture and their differences are rarely questioned. How can seemingly identical colour theories produce completely opposite results?
Mixing colours is simple thanks to the colour wheel
Colour Theory teaches that the colour wheel holds the secrets to mixing colour strategically. And yes, the colour wheel holds up with colour theorist manufactured paints - mixing red and blue paint does make purple; however, these paints are an artificial creation that don't emulate the real world.

Here's an experiment you can try at home: Mix ketchup and mustard together in equal quantities. Did it produce the colour orange? Why not? Read on if you want to know the real reason.

What is Colour Fact?
Colour Fact is a straight-forward, scientific system for modelling the realm of colour.

Unike Colour Theory, Colour Fact was born from an interest in learning the true nature of colour and opposes the use of colour as a form of propaganda or indoctrination. Colour Theory has limited our perception of colour in order to restrain thought not only in art, but also in everyday life.

Colour Fact is a journey of relearning in which the harness of Colour Theory is removed and we discover our innate ability to see and experience colours as they truly exist beyond the colour wheel.

In the following section we outline the six fundamentals of Colour Fact.
1
All colours are independent
More formally: colours are discrete, not continuous. Colour Theory would have you believe that colours are related in a very simplified manner yet this is not true. While it is true that there is an infinite number of colours, each colour is different and incomparable to another. For example, orange is not yellower than red. All three of these colours are separate entities that can't be linked in terms of other colours.

All colours are equal
White holds no special place in the Colour Fact system. Similarly reflective, bright, and transparent colours are in no way different to any other colour.

Go outside and you will see much more than you could in a colour theorist's painting, these are the true colour of the world. Due to the restrictions put in place on our R G B computer screens11, we cannot accurately display images that show many of the colours that exist in reality, so we ask that you seek out examples of reflectivity, brightness, transparency, the various other colours that Colour Theory has taught us not to see.
3
Colours can be identified by their colour capacity and colour volume
Colour capacity is the quality of matter that dictates the colour it can reach when its colour volume reaches its physical limit. Colour capacity is defined by the chemical composition of an object. Colour capacities are generally named after the colour they achieve at full colour volume.

Colour volume is the degree of colour present in an object. Although it is called a volume, it actually represents a number of individual classifications that may seem continuous at first glance, but are separable on closer inspection. Over time, an object's colour volume may increase or decrease but will never go beyond its colour capacity.
Two nails: One brand new, one rusted. A colour theorist would tell you the rusted nail has changed colour. A Fact Society memeber would inform you that both nails are the same colour capacity, but the colour volume of the rusted nail is lower than that of the new one due to exposure to the trasparent colour of air.

Exposure to other colours can change the colour volume of an object. For example, white clothes often increase in colour volume when washed with clothes of other colours. Plastic objects exposed to the bright colour of the sun for long periods of time often change in colour volume12.
4
Black is not a colour
Black exists as a foundation for colour; no colour can exist without first being black and increasing in colour volume from there. Black is what ocurrs when colour volume is zero.

Black as a colour is a concept fabricated by Colour Theory. Dark colours exist but are absolutely independent to black, they do not approximate blackness.
5
Colour capacity is a system that that can be defined at any physical scale.
For example, a single petal of a flower has a certain colour capacity while the flower as a whole has a separate colour capcity. The universe has a colour capacity that naturally fluctuates with time, this is why the prevalence of certain colours has changed throughout history (on Earth and in the Universe).
Did you know that purple was so rare that in Europe, it was a symbol of nobility? It wasn't until the 1850s that humanity's colour capacity changed and purple became much more common. That's why very few flags before the 19th century contain purple.13
Currently the colour associated with the volume that produces purple seems to be on the move again as indicated by Qatari flags fading from purple to maroon.14
6
Colours can be grouped in to colour capacities
Imagine you were trying to describe a measured distance to someone (like 23 kilometres), you would have to say the value of the distance (23) and the unit of measurement used (kilometre). This is akin to how a colour must be referred to by its volume and capacity for someone to know which colour is being referenced with precision. No colour capacity contains all colours and no colour exists exclusively on one colour capacity.
A very common colour capacity (and colour!) is reflective. It includes the colour of mirrors, polished steel, and oranges. Due to colour theory conditioning, oranges may seem to be a sore thumb in this list. The fact is that mirrors and polished steel generally have very high colour volumes, almost to full capacity, whereas oranges' colour is the same colour capacity but with a mid-range colour volume. Establishing which colours belong to a colour capacity is a challenging task that requires observation and/or experimentation under control conditions.

(Remember that transparent can refer to both the colour and the colour capacity!)
